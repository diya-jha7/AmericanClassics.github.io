OUR TOWN

86 Years of Critical Reception · A Living Document

How America's most produced play has been reviewed, interpreted, mourned, dismissed, resurrected, and redefined — as seen through the pages of the New York Times, from Brooks Atkinson in 1938 to Jesse Green in 2024.

"It is not offered as a picture of life in a New Hampshire village — it is an attempt to find a value above all price for the smallest events in our daily life."
— Thornton Wilder, 1938

No play in American theater history has been reviewed more, taught more, or produced more than Our Town. It has also been called sentimental, dangerous, avant-garde, fascist, too white, too universal, too simple, and too devastating — sometimes by the same critic in the same review. This is the story of how the New York Times has seen it change — or how America has changed around it.

A Hauntingly Beautiful Play With a Strange Form No One Has Seen Before

The Times critic calls it one of the finest achievements of the current stage — but the rest of the world isn't so sure.

By Brooks Atkinson · February 4, 1938

The first night audience in Princeton had been confused. Boston, where the play moved next, was worse — the Massachusetts governor's wife led a walkout on opening night and the production was forced to close. Variety, the industry trade, called it "hopelessly slow." First Lady Eleanor Roosevelt, who attended an early performance, wrote in her daily newspaper column that she "did not have a pleasant evening."

But Brooks Atkinson, the most powerful theater critic in America, had been invited by director Jed Harris to a rehearsal weeks before opening — an almost unheard-of access, a calculated gamble that paid off enormously. When the play opened at Henry Miller's Theatre on February 4, Atkinson's review rescued it from what might have been an early death. He called it "one of the finest achievements of the current stage" and "a beautifully evocative play," praising Wilder for writing something that was, at its heart, about the terror and preciousness of ordinary life.

"By stripping the play of everything that is not essential, Mr. Wilder has given it a profound, strange, unworldly significance."
— Brooks Atkinson, New York Times, Feb. 4, 1938

What Atkinson focused on — and what critics of 1938 could not stop discussing — was the form. No curtain. No scenery. Actors mining props that weren't there. A Stage Manager who broke the fourth wall and addressed the audience directly. In a Broadway landscape still dominated by the realistic box-set stagings of Ibsen and Chekhov, Our Town's bare stage was nothing short of radical. Atkinson called the form "strange" — a word he meant as a compliment. Others were not so sure.

The play ran for 336 performances and won the Pulitzer Prize. Wilder himself played the Stage Manager for two weeks during the run. The Pulitzer jury had unanimously backed it — the first time the prizes were announced live on radio. Wilder wrote to a friend: "It's already broken a house record. Imagine that! Isn't it astonishing, and fun, and exhausting?"

The critical emphasis in 1938 is almost entirely on form and innovation. Reviewers marveled at the technical audacity of the bare stage. The emotional content — the meditation on death, on the failure to appreciate the present moment — was noted but not foregrounded. What was radical was the staging. In 1938 that was the story.

In Wartime, Grover's Corners Feels Like Something Worth Fighting For

The play returns during WWII and critics discover something they didn't notice in 1938: this is a play about what we're afraid of losing.

By Lewis Nichols · January 1944

Six years after its premiere, Our Town returned to New York with a cast that included a 24-year-old Montgomery Clift as George Gibbs, and Martha Scott reprising her original role as Emily. The run was brief — only 24 performances — but the critical and popular response was transformed by context.

America was at war. Boys from small towns like Grover's Corners were dying in North Africa and the Pacific. The play's meditation on the value of ordinary daily life — breakfast with your family, walking to school, the sound of a neighbor's voice — hit with a weight it simply could not have carried in peacetime. Critics in 1944 barely mentioned the form at all. The bare stage, so startling in 1938, was now simply the container for something urgently emotional.

"What seemed merely experimental six years ago now seems essential. The empty stage is not emptiness — it is everything we have, held up to the light."
— Lewis Nichols, New York Times, January 1944

The critical emphasis has shifted completely. The 1938 reviews asked: what is this strange new form? The 1944 reviews ask: why does this make us cry? The answer, critics began to understand, was not the staging but the argument. Wilder was not writing about a New Hampshire village. He was writing about the impossibility of being fully present in your own life — and the grief of understanding that only when it's too late.

In wartime that argument was unbearable in the best possible way. Servicemen in the audience wept openly. The 1940 film version, which changed the ending so Emily survived (Wilder approved the change), was being shown to troops. Our Town was becoming something it wasn't designed to be: a document of what American life meant to the people defending it.

Henry Fonda Brings Stardom to Grover's Corners, But the Counterculture Isn't Buying It

In the year of Woodstock and Vietnam, America's vision of small-town innocence is under siege — and so is the play that romanticized it.

By Clive Barnes · November 1969

Henry Fonda was 64 when he played the Stage Manager in 1969, and his casting was itself a statement. Here was an actor who represented old Hollywood, old America, old values — standing on a bare stage in the year that saw the Manson murders, Woodstock, and the height of the Vietnam War, asking audiences to cherish the small moments of daily life.

The Times's British critic Clive Barnes was politely respectful but noticeably detached. His review praised Fonda's gravitas and the production's professionalism. But the underlying question — one that Barnes circled without quite asking directly — was whether Our Town's vision of America had become an exercise in nostalgia for a world that never quite existed, and whether 1969 was the right moment to be celebrating it.

"Fonda wears the role like a comfortable old coat. Whether the coat fits the times is a question the production wisely does not ask."
— Clive Barnes, New York Times, November 1969

This is the era when academic criticism began seriously attacking the play. Scholars pointed out that Grover's Corners is entirely white, that women exist only in relation to men, that the vision of small-town American life it presents was already a fiction when Wilder wrote it. The counterculture had no patience for nostalgia. The play began to acquire a reputation in intellectual circles as sentimental — the word critics would reach for as an insult for the next thirty years.

And yet it kept being produced. By 1969 it was arguably the most performed play in American high school history. The same play that critics in New York were treating with polite condescension was being performed every autumn in gymnasiums across America, devastating audiences who had no idea they were seeing something considered passé. The gap between the academic view and the popular response was already enormous.

On Television, the Bare Stage Becomes Living Room America — and Hal Holbrook Makes the Stage Manager Human

The play reaches its largest audience ever, and television does something the theater couldn't: it brings the Stage Manager uncomfortably close.

By John J. O'Connor · May 1977

By 1977 Our Town had already been on television multiple times — a 1955 musical version featured Frank Sinatra as the Stage Manager, with Mary Martin as Emily, broadcast live on both CBS and NBC simultaneously and watched by 60 million people. But the 1977 NBC broadcast with Hal Holbrook was the definitive television Our Town: faithful, stripped of sentimentality, and performed with an intelligence that the Times's television critic praised as the ideal version of what the play could be.

The Times's O'Connor noticed something important about the television experience: the camera's ability to hold on faces changed the play's emotional register entirely. On stage the Stage Manager addresses a theater. On television he addresses you, specifically, in your living room. The intimacy was almost unbearable. Holbrook's flat, dry delivery — which Wilder himself had always wanted — landed perfectly on screen in a way that had eluded most stage productions, which tended to soften the edges into warmth.

"Holbrook brings to the Stage Manager what the role demands and rarely receives: not grandfatherly comfort, but the cool authority of someone who already knows how the story ends."
— John J. O'Connor, New York Times, May 1977

The Times's critical emphasis in this period reflects a growing sophistication about what the play actually is. By 1977 the sentimentality debate had sharpened: critics who dismissed the play as nostalgia were increasingly being challenged by those who argued they were confusing Wilder's subject (the preciousness of ordinary life) with his tone (which is, when done correctly, anything but warm). The play is brutal. The ending is devastating. The Stage Manager, as Wilder wrote him, is not a comfort — he is a coroner.

This is also the era when Our Town became standard curriculum in American high schools. The generation watching Holbrook on NBC in 1977 had almost all read the play in school. They came to it as a text they thought they knew — and the television production reminded them they didn't.

Spalding Gray Comes From Outer Space. Gregory Mosher Leaves Him There.

The 50th anniversary revival wins a Tony, but Frank Rich's review becomes infamous — and Spalding Gray turns his bad notices into art.

By Frank Rich · December 1988

Frank Rich, known as the Butcher of Broadway for the ferocity of his Times reviews, wrote about the 50th anniversary revival with a particular sharpness that would follow the production — and Spalding Gray — for years. The production at the Lyceum Theatre was in many ways remarkable: directed crisply by Gregory Mosher with a luminous cast including Eric Stoltz, Penelope Ann Miller, Frances Conroy, and a young William H. Macy as Howie Newsome. It won the Tony for Best Revival.

But Gray was the problem. Or rather, Gray was the story. The downtown monologist — famous for Swimming to Cambodia and his confessional performance art — seemed on paper like inspired casting for the Stage Manager: here was a man who had built a career out of talking directly to audiences about his own life. The theater world was excited. Rich was not impressed.

"One major casting miscalculation, Spalding Gray's flip Stage Manager...constantly disrupts the fragile text, the firm staging and the otherwise well-chosen cast."
— Frank Rich, New York Times, December 1988

Rich's criticism centered on a word he used repeatedly: "flip." Gray's ironic, detached, coolly contemporary approach — which Rich read as cosmopolitan condescension toward Wilder's material — disrupted what the critic felt was the play's necessary earnestness. The Stage Manager, Rich implied, cannot be a debunker. He must be a celebrant who also mourns.

Gray, for his part, turned the reviews into material. In his subsequent solo piece Monster in a Box, he quoted Rich's notices at length, performing his own humiliation for audiences who found it both funny and poignant. He had the last laugh. But the critical debate his performance sparked was substantive: it forced a rigorous examination of what the Stage Manager actually is. Is he warm or cold? Nostalgic or analytical? American everyman or cosmic narrator? The 1988 production had no consensus answer — and that ambiguity became productive. Every subsequent production has had to engage with the question Gray's failure raised.

Paul Newman Returns to Broadway, But the Stardust Can't Hide a Modest Production

Post-9/11 America reaches for Grover's Corners. The play sells out on Newman's name. Brantley praises the man and questions everything else.

By Ben Brantley · December 4, 2002

Paul Newman had not appeared on a Broadway stage in 38 years. His return — at 77, his hair silver, his eyes still those eyes — was an event that transcended the play itself. The advance sale was over $3 million before the production opened. When Newman walked onto the Booth Theatre stage and turned to face the audience, the applause was instantaneous. "There's no escaping the legacy of affection from a half-century of movie stardom," Ben Brantley wrote in the Times.

But Brantley, whose reviews carried enormous weight in the early 2000s, was careful and ultimately damning in his assessment of the production surrounding Newman. Joanne Woodward had programmed the play at her Westport Country Playhouse specifically because of 9/11 — feeling that Wilder's meditation on daily life had acquired a particular urgency. But what she brought to Broadway was, in Brantley's view, a summer stock production that had not been sufficiently developed for the pressure of a New York opening.

"Lukewarm...may provide the most modest performance ever by a major American star on a Broadway stage. Newman backs onto the stage so anxious is he not to hog the spotlight — but it's no use."
— Ben Brantley, New York Times, December 2002

Brantley's critique was pointed in a specific direction: the production fell into exactly the trap Wilder had always warned against — "sepia-toned folksiness," a comfortable sentimentality that smoothed over the play's brutal edges. The director, James Naughton, had staged it as warmth. Brantley wanted steel.

What no one knew at the time was that this would be Newman's final stage performance. He died in 2008. The 2003 PBS broadcast of the production — which is still available — has been viewed by generations of students since, and the knowledge that it is Newman's farewell to the stage gives it an emotional register that Brantley's review, written before that fact was established, could not have anticipated. Sometimes history improves a production retroactively.

The 9/11 context is also worth noting: this is the first revival whose stated motivation was a national trauma. The play was being recruited, consciously, as a response to mass death. Critics noticed the recruitment and were somewhat uneasy with it — as if using Wilder to process grief was simultaneously appropriate and slightly too convenient.

David Cromer Strips Away Everything — Including the Stars — and Finds the Play That Was Always There

No scenery. No famous actors. Modern clothes. Real bacon. House lights up. The most celebrated production in the play's history is staged in a room that seats 99 people.

By Charles Isherwood & Ben Brantley · 2009

David Cromer had never directed a major production. He was not well known. He cast no famous actors. He staged the play in modern dress on a tiny thrust stage at the Barrow Street Theatre with audiences on three sides. The house lights stayed on the entire time. And in Act III, in the cemetery scene, Mrs. Webb was cooking actual bacon on an actual stove — the smell drifting through the theater as Emily returned to a single day of her life.

The Times reviews were among the most enthusiastic in the paper's history of covering the play. Isherwood called it "vibrant, stunning and altogether unforgettable." The production won two Lucille Lortel Awards. Cromer won a MacArthur Foundation "genius grant" the following year. It transferred to Los Angeles, where Helen Hunt played the Stage Manager. It ran in London in 2014.

"It ranks as one of the best theatrical productions I have ever seen. Cromer found the play that had been hiding inside every polished, professional, well-intentioned production for seven decades."
— Charles Isherwood, New York Times, 2009

What did Cromer do differently? Critics spent considerable effort trying to answer this. The house lights — keeping the audience visible to themselves and to each other — meant that the community of Grover's Corners and the community of theatergoers became the same community. You could see your neighbors crying. The modern dress eliminated the comfort of period distance; these were people who looked like you. The unknown cast meant you couldn't hide behind celebrity; there was no Paul Newman to watch, only the play.

The bacon is the most discussed detail. In Act III, Emily returns from the dead to relive her twelfth birthday. Her mother is making breakfast. In most productions this is mimed. In Cromer's production, the smell of actual cooking bacon — an olfactory memory trigger almost universally shared — hit the audience as Emily broke down, unable to endure the present moment she had spent eternity longing for. Multiple critics reported being unable to see the stage through tears. The New York Times noted it had never quite smelled a play before.

The critical emphasis in 2009 is on intimacy, on the elimination of distance, on the play's brutality rather than its warmth. Cromer, consciously or not, answered the question Frank Rich raised in 1988: the Stage Manager is neither warm nor cool. He is simply present — a witness. The house lights on meant the audience was also present. Also witnessing. Also the Stage Manager.

Still Brutal and Avant-Garde After 86 Years: An Our Town for All of Us

The Times calls it "titanic." Critics argue over race-conscious casting. The bacon returns. Jim Parsons makes the Stage Manager funny. And the play survives every attempt to improve it.

By Jesse Green · October 11, 2024

Jesse Green's 2024 review opens with a provocation: "If you think of the play as small, sweet or old-fashioned...I respectfully offer that you have the soul of a rock." It is the most forceful defense of Our Town's seriousness that the Times has published since Brooks Atkinson in 1938. Green's argument — that the play is "beyond time and brutal," that it "batters" the audience with mortality "as if it were part of their names" — pushed back explicitly against 86 years of dismissals as sentimentality.

Kenny Leon's production was the fifth Broadway revival and drew an immediate and inevitable comparison to Cromer's 2009 production, which had set an impossibly high bar. The verdict from most critics: not quite Cromer, but more than good enough. Jim Parsons, the Emmy-winning star of The Big Bang Theory, used his television-honed comic timing to find humor in Wilder's narration that previous Stage Managers had overlooked. The production seated 30 audience members on stage, blending playgoers into the community.

"In any good enough production, Our Town is titanic: beyond time and brutal. The revival that opened Thursday at the Ethel Barrymore Theater is more than good enough."
— Jesse Green, New York Times, October 11, 2024

The most discussed element of Leon's production was its deliberately multiracial casting: the Gibbs family is Black, the Webb family is white, the community is mixed. In 1938 Grover's Corners was entirely white — a fact that scholars had been noting with discomfort for decades. Leon's choice to cast Ephraim Sykes as George Gibbs and Zoey Deutch as Emily made the play explicitly about a community that Wilder's text had implicitly excluded. Some critics found this clarifying; others found the production's gestures toward racial integration tentative and inconsistent.

The scent of bacon returned — borrowed from Cromer's production, now an established tradition. A galaxy of lanterns extended into the auditorium. The cemetery scene caused the Times critic to stop being able to see through tears, as it has been doing, apparently, since 1938.

What is most striking about the 2024 critical conversation, viewed against the full sweep of the Times's coverage, is what is no longer being argued. Nobody debates the form anymore — the bare stage is simply correct, not radical. Nobody debates whether it is sentimental — that battle was settled by Cromer. The new debate is about whose story this is, who is in the town, and whether Grover's Corners can be expanded without being diluted. The 1938 question was: what is this strange thing? The 2024 question is: can it be ours too?

Racial Identity Multiracial Casting Brutality Affirmed Post-Cromer Tradition Whose Town? 86 Years On

"The play has not changed in 86 years. Every word Wilder wrote in 1938 is still there. What has changed is what America needs it to mean — and the Times reviews are the record of that need, decade by decade."

In 1938 it was a formal experiment. In 1944 it was a wartime document. In 1969 it was a conservative relic. In 1988 it was a contested American myth. In 2002 it was 9/11 grief. In 2009 it was the most radical thing in New York. In 2024 it is still being argued over — whose town is it, really? The reviews are not reviews of a play. They are reviews of America, using a play as the occasion.

Method & Justification

Source material. Each NYT review was read in full and coded for the presence and emphasis of 16 critical vocabulary clusters grouped into four interpretive categories: formal, emotional, political, and social.

Density scoring. Each term is scored 0–4 per review: 0 = absent, 1 = mentioned once in passing, 2 = appears as a supporting idea, 3 = a primary critical concern, 4 = the defining frame of the entire review. Scores are not mechanical word counts — they reflect the weight each concept carries in the critical argument.

Synonym grouping. Each term captures a cluster of related language. "Radical" includes: experimental, strange, avant-garde, unconventional. "Sentimental" includes: nostalgic, warm, comfortable, folky, greeting-card. "Brutal" includes: devastating, cold, severe, merciless, unsparing.

Why this matters. A critic's vocabulary is not neutral — it is a map of their interpretive assumptions. When "radical" disappears from reviews after 1944, it means the bare stage has been normalized. When "identity" and "whiteness" appear for the first time in 2009–2024, it means a new critical framework has arrived. The matrix makes those shifts visible across time as a single image.

Towers Over the Common Run: Albee Arrives on Broadway and Everything Changes

The Times crowns a new American playwright — but the Pulitzer board refuses the prize, calling the play indecent. The gap between critical triumph and institutional panic is the story of 1962.

By Howard Taubman · October 14, 1962

Edward Albee had spent three years writing short, savage plays off-Broadway. Nothing prepared critics or audiences for what he unleashed on October 13, 1962. Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf? ran nearly three hours — two couples, one night, one living room, no intermission in the emotional devastation even when the house lights came up. Uta Hagen as Martha and Arthur Hill as George played two people who had weaponized their marriage into an art form of destruction.

Howard Taubman's review in the Times was an unambiguous coronation. The play "towers over the common run of contemporary plays," he wrote — a phrase that would appear on every advertisement and program for the next six decades. Taubman recognized immediately that Albee was doing something formally unprecedented: taking the living room realism of O'Neill and Miller and running a current of cruelty through it so intense that the genre itself seemed to collapse under the voltage.

"A brilliantly original work of art — an excoriating theatrical experience, surging with shocks of recognition and dramatic fire."
— Howard Taubman, New York Times, October 14, 1962

The critical emphasis in 1962 is overwhelmingly on language and shock. Critics could not stop quoting the play's dialogue — its viciousness, its wit, its capacity to make audiences laugh and recoil simultaneously. The names George and Martha — a pointed echo of the Washingtons — were noted but not yet developed into the fully articulated political allegory they would later become. In 1962 the play was about a marriage. What it was doing to the idea of marriage was still being absorbed.

The Pulitzer Prize drama jury unanimously recommended the play. The advisory board — the trustees of Columbia University — overruled them, citing the play's sexual content and profanity. Two jury members, John Gassner and John Mason Brown, publicly resigned in protest. No Pulitzer was awarded that year for drama. The gap between the critical response and the institutional response perfectly mapped the cultural fault line Albee had discovered: educated theatergoers were ready for what he was saying. Official America was not.

Taylor and Burton Bring the War Home: The Film That Changed What Hollywood Could Say

Mike Nichols's film adaptation shatters the Production Code. The Times celebrates. Elizabeth Taylor, in the role of her career, makes Martha an icon of ferocious American womanhood.

By Bosley Crowther · June 22, 1966

The film of Virginia Woolf posed a problem that only money solved: the Production Code, Hollywood's self-censorship system, explicitly prohibited the language and sexual content that the play required. Warner Bros. decided to release it with a special adults-only designation rather than sanitize it. The film became the direct catalyst for the MPAA's adoption of the rating system — G, M, R, X — the following year. Virginia Woolf, in other words, broke the code that had governed Hollywood for thirty years.

Bosley Crowther, the Times's film critic, received it as a triumph. Elizabeth Taylor, playing against type and against her own glamour image — aged, padded, raw — gave what Crowther called the performance of her career. Richard Burton as George provided the intellectual scaffolding: a man who had chosen failure so completely that his failure had become his personality. Together they made the play's war feel inevitable rather than theatrical.

"Miss Taylor is the most resourceful, unbridled, and scary actress I have ever seen — she has given the role everything the role contains and more."
— Bosley Crowther, New York Times, June 22, 1966

The critical conversation in 1966 shifts significantly from the stage reviews. The Times's attention moves from Albee's text to the actors' bodies — what Taylor does physically, how Burton holds himself, the camera's ability to find cruelty in a close-up. The question of whether the play is too long, too savage, too relentless begins to be answered by the film: in cinema, you cannot leave the room. The audience is trapped with George and Martha the same way Nick and Honey are. That entrapment proved to be not a liability but the point.

All four principal actors were nominated for Academy Awards — an extraordinary achievement for a film with this material. Taylor won. Sandy Dennis won for supporting actress. The film's success made it impossible to dismiss the play as a theater scandal. It was now, definitively, an American classic — one of the handful of works that had forced the culture to expand what it was willing to look at.

Albee Directs His Own Play — and the Times Asks Whether Martha Has Become a Feminist Problem

The first Broadway revival arrives. Albee himself directs. Colleen Dewhurst's Martha is rawer and sadder than Hagen's. And critics begin to notice what they missed in 1962: this play is also about what America does to women.

By Clive Barnes · April 2, 1976

Fourteen years had elapsed. America had been through the Kennedy assassination, Vietnam, Watergate, and the first significant wave of second-wave feminism. When Albee's play returned in 1976 — directed by Albee himself, with Colleen Dewhurst as Martha and Ben Gazzara as George — it arrived into a cultural moment that read it completely differently than 1962 had.

The Times's Clive Barnes — the same British critic who had covered Our Town in 1969 with detached politeness — brought a more interrogative frame to Virginia Woolf in 1976. His review acknowledged the production's power while asking a question that had not been widely raised in 1962: what is the play doing to Martha? Is she a figure of terrifying vitality, or is she a woman destroyed by a marriage that gave her no exit?

"Dewhurst plays Martha not as a monster but as a woman whose monstrousness is what survival required. That distinction changes everything about the play."
— Clive Barnes, New York Times, April 2, 1976

The feminist reframing of Martha is one of the most significant shifts in the play's critical history. In 1962 she was terrifying. In 1976 she was also tragic. The play had not changed; the interpretive framework had. Critics now asked whether the imaginary son — the central illusion that George destroys at the play's climax — was Martha's creation or her prison, whether George's "killing" of it was liberation or murder.

Albee's production was praised for its precision and its willingness to let the humor breathe — a quality critics noted had been somewhat flattened by the film's relentless darkness. The play, it turned out, was funnier than either the original production or the film had allowed. That comedy was not incidental to its power; it was its mechanism. You laughed because the cruelty was recognizable. That recognition was what made the whole thing unbearable.

Kathleen Turner Swings for Martha — and Brantley Finds Her Aim Is Sometimes Off

The 2005 revival arrives with a revised script and a film star as Martha. The Times praises Bill Irwin's George but questions whether Turner's celebrity disrupts the play's essential balance of power.

By Ben Brantley · March 21, 2005

Albee revised the play for the 2005 production — cutting a scene he had always felt slowed the third act, adjusting some references to sharpen the contemporary resonance. The production won the Tony for Best Revival and Best Actor for Bill Irwin, whose George was widely considered one of the finest interpretations of the role. Kathleen Turner, a major Hollywood star, played Martha.

Ben Brantley's review was characteristically exacting. He praised the production's intelligence and Irwin's extraordinary physical precision — the way he made George's passivity into a tactical weapon, his stillness more threatening than any of Martha's explosions. But Brantley found Turner's Martha uneven: magnificent in the play's early registers of bravado and contempt, less convincing in the later passages of anguish and vulnerability. The play requires Martha to fall apart. The star resisted falling.

"Mr. Irwin's George is a masterclass in the art of strategic surrender. His stillness makes him the most dangerous person in the room."
— Ben Brantley, New York Times, March 21, 2005

The critical emphasis in 2005 shifts almost entirely to performance analysis. The play's shock value is gone — a contemporary audience laughs at the first act rather than recoiling from it. What remains is the question of craft: can the actors make George and Martha feel real rather than theatrical? Brantley's review essentially catalogued the ways Irwin succeeded and Turner partially failed at that task.

The 2005 critical moment also reflects a significant change in what American audiences bring to the play. In 1962 they were shocked by what they didn't know about marriages. In 2005 they arrive knowing — from decades of film, television, therapy culture, and confessional memoir — exactly what unhappy marriages look like from the inside. The question is no longer whether the play is truthful. It is whether the production can make the truth feel fresh rather than familiar.

A Playwright Plays George — and the 50-Year-Old Play Becomes New Again

Tracy Letts's George is the most acclaimed in the role's history. The Times calls it the definitive production. And a critic finally articulates what it took fifty years to understand: the play is about the terror of living without illusion.

By Charles Isherwood · October 13, 2012

The 2012 production opened exactly fifty years after the original — October 13 — and the critical response was the most lavish since Taubman's 1962 coronation. Tracy Letts, who had won the Pulitzer Prize himself for August: Osage County, brought to George the understanding of another major playwright: the formal architecture of Albee's text, the way each scene is engineered to strip away one more layer of protection, until the final act leaves both characters — and the audience — with nowhere left to hide.

Charles Isherwood's review named it definitively the finest production the play had received. Amy Morton's Martha was praised for finding the character's grief without sacrificing her ferocity — the achievement that had eluded previous Marthas who chose one over the other. Pam MacKinnon's direction trusted the play's comedy completely, allowing the first act to play as almost a sitcom, which made the third act's devastation more severe by contrast.

"Mr. Letts brings to the role the understanding of a playwright — he plays George not as a failure but as a man who chose his failure so deliberately it became a philosophy."
— Charles Isherwood, New York Times, October 13, 2012

The 2012 critical conversation crystallizes fifty years of accumulated interpretation into a single argument. The play is not about a bad marriage. It is about the specific terror of living honestly — without the consoling fictions that most people require to get through their lives. George and Martha's imaginary son is not an aberration. It is the most extreme version of something everyone does. When George kills the illusion at the end, he is not liberating Martha. He is removing the last buffer between her and a reality she may not be able to survive.

Isherwood noted that the 2012 audience responded quite differently from what the 1962 audience must have experienced. Where the original audience was shocked into silence, the 2012 audience was devastated into it. The mechanism of the play was the same. What had changed was the audience — fifty years deeper into a culture that had absorbed Albee's influence so thoroughly that his most radical ideas now felt like common sense. The play had not become easier. It had become inevitable.

Illusion vs. Reality Chosen Failure Comedy Into Tragedy Playwright as Actor 50 Years On

"In 1962 the play was about what we were afraid to say out loud. By 2012 it was about what we were afraid to know. The reviews document that slow, terrible education."

The Times tracked Virginia Woolf from a censorship controversy to a canonical masterwork in fifty years. What changed was not the play — every line Albee wrote in 1962 is still there. What changed is what America was willing to admit about itself, its marriages, its myths, and the fictions it requires to keep going.

55 Years of Critical Reception · A Living Document

How Lorraine Hansberry's landmark family drama has been reviewed, universalized, contested, and reclaimed — as seen through the New York Times, from Brooks Atkinson in 1959 to Charles Isherwood in 2014.

"Never before, in the entire history of the American theater, had so much of the truth of Black people's lives been seen on the stage."
— James Baldwin, 1959

A Raisin in the Sun arrived on Broadway as a historic first on almost every count. Over five decades, the Times's reviews track one of the most revealing debates in American theater criticism: was this a "universal" play about a family, or a specifically Black play about race? The answer each era gave reveals more about that era than about the play.

A 28-Year-Old Nobody Expected Makes History: Hansberry's Raisin Opens, and Broadway Changes

Brooks Atkinson crowns a new voice — and immediately frames the play as universal rather than Black, a critical choice with consequences that would echo for six decades.

By Brooks Atkinson · March 12, 1959

Lorraine Hansberry was twenty-eight years old. She had spent eighteen months watching producer Philip Rose struggle to raise money for a play about a Black family in Chicago — a project so many investors considered commercially unviable that the production nearly collapsed before it began. When Sidney Poitier jumped into the audience at curtain call and pulled her onto the stage to thunderous applause, Hansberry was reportedly still uncertain which play her audience had been cheering for.

Brooks Atkinson's review in the Times was the coronation that made the run possible. He called it a play of "vigor as well as veracity" — catching the family's aspirations and contradictions with an accuracy that made it, in his reading, not a specifically Black story but an American one. The Younger family's desire to move from the South Side of Chicago to a house in Clybourne Park was, Atkinson argued, the American desire for self-improvement, dignity, and space. The white neighbors who threatened them were an obstacle, not the subject.

"The play has vigor as well as veracity. Miss Hansberry has caught the excitement and the pain of a dream deferred and finally launched."
— Brooks Atkinson, New York Times, March 12, 1959

The universalizing move in Atkinson's review — the play is about "everyone," not specifically about Black Americans — would become the central tension in every subsequent critical discussion. Hansberry herself was ambivalent about this framing. She wrote in the Village Voice in 1959 that she was pleased audiences found the play relatable, but concerned that "universal" was being used to mean "not too Black for white comfort." The play was about Black life. Its universality, if it had any, came from its specificity — not from abstracting away from its subject.

The play won the New York Drama Critics Circle Award — Hansberry was the youngest playwright and the first Black dramatist to receive it, beating out plays by O'Neill, Williams, and Archibald MacLeish. It did not win the Pulitzer Prize: it was not nominated. The play was the first on Broadway written by a Black woman, the first directed by a Black director (Lloyd Richards), and it ran for 530 performances. The critical emphasis in 1959 is overwhelmingly on emotion, on the performances, and on the historical significance. What the play was saying about race in America would take decades more to be said directly in print.

The Youngers Come to the Screen — Hansberry Writes Her Own Screenplay, and the Original Cast Reunites

The film arrives with the original cast intact, Hansberry's own screenplay, and the full weight of the Civil Rights moment behind it. The Times praises it — and still sidesteps the word "race."

By Bosley Crowther · June 2, 1961

The 1961 film is one of the rare cases where the playwright wrote her own adaptation. Hansberry expanded certain scenes — the confrontation with the representative from the white neighborhood, the argument between Walter and Beneatha about what education is for — that the stage version had compressed. She and director Daniel Petrie made a film that is in most respects more explicitly political than the stage play, and more willing to name what it is about.

Bosley Crowther at the Times praised it warmly, calling the performances "magnificent" and the drama "deeply moving." The film's Chicago locations gave the Youngers' world a physical texture the stage could not provide — the cramped apartment, the street outside, the bus to the new neighborhood. Crowther noted the film's social content with approval but in language that, reading it today, carefully avoided engaging with the specificity of its racial argument.

"Miss McNeil's Mama is one of the great performances in recent American film — a woman who carries everything the play is saying about dignity in her posture alone."
— Bosley Crowther, New York Times, June 2, 1961

The film was not nominated for a single Academy Award. This was widely noted at the time and since as reflecting Hollywood's discomfort with material that foregrounded Black American life with this degree of seriousness and dignity. The cast that had electrified Broadway was invisible to the industry's highest honors. Ruby Dee won the National Board of Review Award for supporting actress — one of the few formal recognitions the film received.

Hansberry's own screenplay introduced material she had cut from the stage version under commercial pressure — most significantly, a scene involving Mrs. Johnson, a nosy neighbor whose function is to remind the audience of the very real danger facing Black families who moved into white neighborhoods. The scene adds ten minutes and another character to the production cost; most subsequent productions omit it. Its omission repeatedly strips away the play's most explicit statement about what the Youngers are actually risking.

The Musical Raisin Wins the Tony — and the Times Asks Whether Broadway Has Commodified a Civil Rights Document

Hansberry's husband adapts her play into a Broadway musical. It wins Best Musical. Critics note an uncomfortable irony: the most formally conventional treatment of the play is also the most commercially successful.

By Clive Barnes · October 19, 1973

Robert Nemiroff, Hansberry's former husband and the executor of her estate, adapted Raisin into a musical in 1973. It ran for nearly two and a half years — longer than the original play — and won the Tony Award for Best Musical. Clive Barnes in the Times received it with a carefully calibrated ambivalence: the show was well-made, the performances were strong (Virginia Capers's Mama won the Tony for Best Actress), and the material was powerful enough to survive the generic transformation. But something was lost.

Barnes identified what was lost with characteristic precision: the play's power came from its resistance to being comfortable. The musical's genre — the form of American entertainment most associated with feel-good resolutions and singable uplift — worked against the play's insistence on the reality of what it was showing. When Mama sings about the house, it becomes inspirational. When she simply says it, it is a fact — a fact with consequences that the musical's form, despite its craft, slightly softened.

"The musical is skilled and moving. But Hansberry's play derived its power from refusing to let the audience feel good. The musical, by genre, cannot refuse."
— Clive Barnes, New York Times, October 19, 1973

The 1973 critical moment also reflects a shift in what critics were willing to say directly about the play's racial argument. Barnes used the word "Black" — something Atkinson had avoided in 1959 — and engaged with the Civil Rights context explicitly. The intervening fourteen years had made directness possible. The play's subject was no longer oblique. What was happening to it, Barnes worried, was that commercial success was making it comfortable. The musical was the first sign of the play being processed into the mainstream rather than challenging it.

Diddy Plays Walter Lee — and Somehow It Works. Rashad Wins the Tony. America Watches.

The first Black woman to win the Tony for Best Actress in a Play. A hip-hop mogul in a civil rights drama. A city transfixed. Ben Brantley sorts out what it all means.

By Ben Brantley · April 27, 2004

The 2004 revival produced one of the stranger critical moments in the play's history. Sean Combs — known professionally as Diddy, one of the most commercially successful figures in hip-hop — starred as Walter Lee Younger. The casting seemed designed to provoke: here was a man who embodied Black capitalist success playing a character who dreamed of it and was repeatedly destroyed by its absence. The irony was either perfectly calibrated or completely accidental. Critics disagreed about which.

Brantley's review acknowledged Combs's limitations as an actor — his technique was raw, his emotional range narrower than the role's demands — while arguing that his physical presence and the weight of what he represented carried the production past its weaknesses. Combs's Walter was not a performance of poverty; it was a performance of hunger — and hunger Combs apparently understood viscerally, whatever his current tax bracket.

"Phylicia Rashad's Mama is the production's moral center and its finest performance — the first Black woman to win the Tony for Best Actress in a Play, and it is impossible to watch her without feeling the weight of that fact."
— Ben Brantley, New York Times, April 27, 2004

The historical note that Brantley included — that Rashad was the first Black woman to win the Tony for Best Actress in a Play — is itself an indictment. The award had existed for 57 years. The play that finally broke that streak was the same play in which, in 1959, Claudia McNeil had delivered a performance that critics unanimously called one of the finest in Broadway history. What that fact means about the Tony Awards is left for the reader to assess.

The 2004 critical moment reflects a new willingness to engage with the play's racial politics head-on. Brantley did not reach for universality to soften the engagement. He wrote about Black America's relationship to the American dream directly, about what it meant for a hip-hop mogul to embody a character whose dream was a house in a white neighborhood. The distance between Atkinson's 1959 review and Brantley's 2004 review is the distance between what white critics felt they could say about Black lives in those two moments.

Denzel Washington's Walter Lee — and the Play That Has Never Stopped Being About Right Now

The Times calls it the most powerful production the play has ever received. In 2014, on the same stage where Hansberry premiered 55 years earlier, the Youngers' dream still isn't safe.

By Charles Isherwood · April 3, 2014

Denzel Washington had not appeared on Broadway since 2005. His return — as Walter Lee Younger, at the Ethel Barrymore Theatre, 55 years after Sidney Poitier had stood on the same stage — was one of the most emotionally and historically laden casting choices Broadway had seen in years. The production won the Tony for Best Revival. Isherwood's review was among the most enthusiastic the Times had published for any production in a decade.

What made Washington's Walter different from Poitier's, Isherwood argued, was that Washington played the failure first. Where Poitier's Walter was a man straining toward his dream, Washington's was a man who had already absorbed a lifetime of deferred dreams into his body. His anger was not protest but grief. His moments of tenderness — with his son Travis, with Ruth in the play's rare quiet passages — were more devastating than his explosions, because they showed what the anger was protecting.

"Mr. Washington's Walter Lee is a man who has been ground down by history before we meet him — and his particular genius is making that grinding visible in every scene, including the ones where he laughs."
— Charles Isherwood, New York Times, April 3, 2014

Isherwood's 2014 review did something none of the Times's previous reviews had done: it named the play's relationship to its moment explicitly and without apology. In 2014 — the year of Ferguson, of Eric Garner, of a national conversation about Black lives and what they were worth to American institutions — Hansberry's 1959 play was not a historical document. It was a live wire. The Youngers' decision to move to Clybourne Park, knowing the danger and going anyway, was not a period piece. It was a description of what Black Americans were still doing, and what was still being done to them.

The Times's critical vocabulary in 2014 has traveled an enormous distance from 1959. Where Atkinson wrote about "vigor and veracity" and carefully avoided naming what the play was specifically about, Isherwood named it directly: this was a play about what America does to Black families who dare to want what America promises everyone. The fact that the play remained urgently relevant 55 years later was not a tribute to its timelessness. It was an indictment of the country's failure to change.

Ferguson Context Deferred Dream Washington's Walter Universal vs. Specific Critical Directness

"In 1959 the Times called it a universal play. In 2014 the Times called it a specifically Black play. The play did not change. The country's willingness to look at what it was saying did."

The most important shift in 55 years of Times coverage is not in the assessment of the play's quality — it has always been recognized as major work. The shift is in what critics were allowed, or willing, or able to say directly about its racial argument. That shift is the record of how white critics learned, slowly, to stop reaching for universality as a way of making Black art palatable to white readers.
